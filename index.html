¬ß1 Introduction

Small and Medium Scale Manufacturing Enterprises (MSMEs) form the backbone of metal manufacturing in regions such as Punjab, Haryana, and Rajasthan. These units commonly produce sheet-metal components, brackets, plates, and fabricated parts for automotive, agricultural, and general engineering applications. Surface quality of these components is a critical factor ‚Äî visible defects such as scratches significantly reduce product value and may lead to rejection by downstream manufacturers.

In most MSME metal workshops, surface inspection is still performed manually by human operators. This process is subjective, inconsistent across shifts, and prone to errors due to fatigue, poor lighting conditions, and high production pressure. Minor defects such as scratches are often missed, while acceptable parts may be unnecessarily rejected, resulting in material wastage and increased production cost.

Large-scale industries address this problem using Automated Optical Inspection (AOI) systems, which rely on specialized hardware, multi-camera setups, and sophisticated sensing technologies. However, such systems are prohibitively expensive and operationally complex for MSMEs. As a result, there exists a significant gap between the inspection capabilities of large industries and small manufacturing units.

Project Goal: Bridge this gap by developing a low-cost, camera-based surface defect screening system using classical computer vision techniques ‚Äî focusing on detecting surface scratches under real-world MSME constraints such as limited data, variable lighting, and minimal infrastructure.

¬ß2 Problem Statement
Manual inspection of metal surfaces in small-scale manufacturing units is unreliable and inconsistent, leading to undetected surface scratches and quality degradation. Existing automated inspection systems are expensive and impractical for MSMEs. The problem addressed in this project is the development of a low-cost, classical computer vision‚Äìbased system capable of detecting surface scratches on metal components using standard camera images, thereby enabling early-stage defect screening suitable for MSME environments.

¬ß3 Literature Review

Surface defect detection has been extensively studied in large-scale industrial manufacturing. High-end AOI systems deployed in steel rolling mills, automotive stamping units, and electronics manufacturing use laser scanning, structured light, near-infrared imaging, or deep learning‚Äìbased vision models to achieve micron-level precision and high-speed inspection. While effective, such solutions require controlled environments, expensive hardware, and skilled maintenance personnel ‚Äî making them unsuitable for small-scale workshops.

Earlier research relied heavily on classical techniques including edge detection, texture analysis, and statistical pattern recognition. Methods based on Canny edge detection, Gabor filters, Local Binary Patterns (LBP), and Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) were commonly used for detecting scratches, cracks, and texture anomalies on metal surfaces. Dimensionality reduction and classification were typically performed using PCA, LDA, or distance-based classifiers.

Gap identified: Despite the availability of advanced inspection technologies, there is limited work focusing on affordable, explainable, and lightweight defect screening systems tailored specifically for small-scale metal manufacturing units. The NEU Surface Defect Dataset contains only defective samples, without defect-free reference images ‚Äî further motivating the need for a self-collected normal class. This project builds upon classical CV principles to address this gap directly.
Canny Edge Detection
Widely used for detecting linear discontinuities on surfaces. Highlights scratch patterns as elongated edge clusters, forming the basis for scratch-specific segmentation.
Gabor Filters
Multi-orientation texture filters effective for detecting directional patterns. Scratches at any orientation can be captured by a well-designed Gabor filter bank.
LBP & HOG
Rotation-invariant texture and gradient-based descriptors proven effective for surface anomaly characterization without requiring large training sets.
PCA + LDA
Dimensionality reduction and discriminative projection methods that generalize well on small labeled datasets ‚Äî a key advantage over deep learning in MSME settings.
